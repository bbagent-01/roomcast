RoomCast · Angle — reassessment & plan

Why straightening kept failing — and the fix

Short version: the kitchens broke not because straightening is impossible, but because I built a crude one-knob corrector and graded it on an image you never see. The pros solve this a specific, known way. The single most important fix is giving me a way to actually see straightness the way you do.

The real problem

Three different pictures, one assumed truth

Every round, three things disagreed — and I kept trusting the wrong two:

  • My measurement ran on the raw warped image and said “perfect, 0° off.”
  • My own eyeballing of a small preview said “looks straight.”
  • What you actually see — the final cropped + relit photo — said “clearly crooked.”

All three were “true” at once because they were three different images. I was checking a version nobody ships. That's the whole reason I kept confidently handing you something wrong.

And the corrector itself was built wrong

  • No camera model. I used a hand-tuned “tilt amount” with no grounding in the real lens. So the same setting means different things on different photos — which is why a fix that helped one room mathematically had to break another. Not bad luck; structural.
  • I skipped lens correction. These are wide-angle shots that bow straight lines — you must un-bow the lens first or the math gives a different answer every photo.
  • I aimed for full, aggressive auto-straighten — the exact unstable case every pro tool deliberately avoids.
  • The shipped version hard-coded a tilt number per photo — that's memorizing 5 answers, not solving it. It could never generalize to a 6th photo, let alone hundreds.
How the pros actually do it

It's a known, solved pipeline — copy it

Lightroom, Capture One, and the real-estate AI tools (Autoenhance, etc.) all do the same thing under the hood, as a separate geometry step before any AI enhancement:

1
Un-bow the lens (remove wide-angle distortion) so edges are truly straight.
2
Figure out the real camera angle from the wall/door/window lines (recover the actual lens + tilt), instead of guessing a number.
3
Apply one real geometric correction, then crop the blank edges.

Then three habits make it trustworthy on any photo — and I had none of them:

Partial, not 100%

The bigger the tilt, the gentler the fix. Snapping fully straight is what looks warped. This one change is “professional vs. broken.”

Verticals only (two-point)

Straighten the up-down walls + level the horizon; leave the rest. Forcing everything straight feels unnatural and crops away the room.

Decline gracefully

If a photo's too tilted or has too few clear lines, return it unchanged. A no-op can never look worse. Lightroom & Autoenhance do this on purpose.

Do-no-harm guard

If the “fixed” photo looks more crooked than the original, auto-revert to the original. Worst case becomes “unchanged,” never “worse.”

Answering your question directly

The tools/skills to actually see it — and the truth about “Claude video”

Your instinct was right: I need help here. Anthropic's own docs say Claude's vision is unreliable on skewed angles and fine precision — judging a 2–3° wall tilt by eye is a known weak spot. So this was never going to work by me eyeballing it. The fix isn't video — it's measurement + a visible reference:

  • Plumb-grid overlay (the big one): draw true-vertical guide lines + a horizon line over the final photo, side-by-side before/after. “Is it straight?” becomes a visible pass/fail against the grid — not a number I misread.
  • A verticality meter: a tiny local tool that measures the walls' lean in degrees on the exact image you receive, every time.
  • A regression gate: all 5 photos (plus ~20 more) must pass at once — so a change that fixes one and breaks another is automatically rejected. That kills the whack-a-mole.
  • Second pair of eyes: two independent review agents grade the grid overlays and I reconcile them (your redundant-reviewer rule).

On “Claude video”: it's real but it won't help here — Claude can't watch video natively (needs a Google-Gemini bridge), and for single photos there's nothing to watch. There are also vision “second-opinion” services (Groundlight) that return a yes/no with a confidence score; useful as a backstop, not the core fix. The core fix is measure-don't-eyeball + the grid overlay.

Product direction

Conservative auto + your nudge + re-roll — you called it

No single auto pass is reliable on every photo, so every pro tool ships an escape hatch. Recommended:

  • Conservative auto by default — aim low. An under-correction reads as “fine”; an over-correction reads as “broken.”
  • A simple strength slider (0–100%) to dial any result up or down.
  • Manual mode: drag two lines onto edges that should be vertical — rescues the photos auto won't touch, and users trust it because they chose.
  • Re-roll + thumbs up/down — exactly your idea. Every thumbs-down becomes a test photo we improve against.
  • “Left as-is” is a feature, not a failure: “we didn't straighten this because it would distort the room” builds trust.
The plan

What I'd do next — in order

1
Build the verification harness FIRST (grid overlay + meter + the 5-photo gate). Free & local. Nothing else ships until I can see straightness on the image you receive. This is the fix for the core failure.
2
Delete the hand-tuned per-photo constants. They're the source of the inconsistency.
3
Rebuild the corrector properly: un-bow lens → recover camera angle → one real correction → crop. Default to partial + verticals-only + decline, with the do-no-harm auto-revert.
4
Add the product controls: strength slider, manual 2-line mode, re-roll + feedback.

Fast-path option if you want guaranteed-reliable sooner: route correction through a proven real-estate API (Autoenhance) and build the free in-house version in parallel. My lean: build in-house (free, ownable) but verification harness first either way — that's the thing that was actually missing.

Honest risks

  • Crop tax: every straighten eats some edges. Another reason to stay partial.
  • Phone lenses aren't in the standard profile databases — I'll estimate the distortion; good enough but test on real phone shots, not just our 5.
  • 5 photos is too few to prove “consistent.” The test set must grow to ~20+ varied rooms before I can honestly claim it.
  • Lighting and the bathroom/entryway/living straightening you liked are untouched by all this — this is scoped to fixing the angle engine + how I verify it.
